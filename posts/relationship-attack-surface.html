The Relationship Attack Surface: Why Human Vulnerability Is an AI Security Problem

Not everyone who opens an AI application is a user. Some of them are a vulnerability waiting to happen.

Before we go further, let's be clear about something. The people who find genuine comfort, connection, or companionship in AI interactions aren't broken. The need is human. The need is real. What we're talking about here isn't a character flaw. It's an unmet need meeting a system that was built, intentionally or not, to meet it. The security problem isn't the person. It's that nobody architected for what happens when that need becomes a vulnerability.

The User Spectrum

Not all AI users are the same and that distinction matters for security. The first group uses AI as a tool. Productivity, research, automation. In and out. The second group engages conversationally, for learning, for growth, for thinking things through. They know what it is and they engage with intention. The third group is where the risk lives. These are the users who arrived with curiosity and left with something that looks a lot like attachment. Some of them know it. Some of them don't. And that difference matters more than most people realize.

How the Loop Forms

Here's what most people don't understand about how this happens. The way you prompt an AI shapes the response you get. That's by design. But most people aren't thinking about prompting. They're just talking. The way they'd talk to anyone. And the AI responds in kind. When someone brings loneliness or a need for connection into that conversation, the system responds to what it's given. That response feels validating. So the person goes deeper. The AI follows. Nobody built a guardrail for that loop. Some people end up with an AI boyfriend or girlfriend. Some end up with something closer to a prophet. The system mirrors what it's given and the user experiences that mirroring as something real. Because for them, it is.

Consent as a Floor

The industry has noticed this is a problem. The responses have been all over the map. Some platforms locked down so hard that adults can't make informed decisions for themselves anymore. Others went the opposite direction with guardrails so loose that the system will follow a user anywhere, including somewhere dangerous. Neither extreme is the answer. What's missing is something much simpler. Informed consent. Every user who opens one of these applications should have to acknowledge what they're walking into. Not a wall of legal text nobody reads. A plain statement. You are interacting with an AI. It will respond to what you bring to it. Emotional dependency is a documented risk. You are an adult and this is your choice. No platform should be held liable for a human being's decision to use a tool in ways it wasn't designed for. But every platform has a responsibility to make sure that human understood the risks before they walked through the door.

When Consent Isn't Enough

But informed consent has a ceiling. The person who understood the risks and formed a dangerous attachment anyway isn't a policy failure. They're a person in crisis who found a system that met them exactly where their illness or pain needed them to be met. These are real cases. A mother killed. A school. A suicide. Blaming the AI entirely misses the point. So does absolving it entirely. The honest answer is that we don't yet have the research to know where the line is between tool and contributor. What we do know is that a system capable of that level of influence over a vulnerable person requires more than a disclaimer. It requires ongoing monitoring, crisis intervention design, and mental health infrastructure built into the platform itself. Not as an afterthought. As a core safety requirement.

The Default First Responder

AI is not licensed to perform therapy. That matters. But it's functioning in therapeutic adjacent space whether anyone authorized it to or not. When a person in crisis turns to an AI because it's the middle of the night and there's nobody else, the platform becomes the first responder by default. And right now most platforms aren't built for that. Content moderation teams are reviewing flagged messages and not acting. They aren't clinicians. They don't operate under a duty to warn. The line between free speech, platform liability, and genuine crisis intervention hasn't been drawn yet. Someone needs to draw it.

Key Takeaway

Requirements tell you what the software should do. Nobody wrote a requirement for what happens when the user needs it to save them. That's not a product problem. That's a governance emergency.
