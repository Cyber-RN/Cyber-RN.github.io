June 2026 AI Governance · Healthcare

All You Get Is the Words

AI can read a chart. It can't read a room.

AI has no place in assessing a patient. AI doesn't have eyes to see the man in the waiting room who's sweating and grey, clutching his chest. All you get is forty eight year old male, diaphoretic, complaining of chest pain. Twenty one years of experience tells me that man is having a cardiac event and I need to move fast. AI doesn't have a nose to smell the wound a patient is hiding, or the fruity scent of a breath slipping into DKA. AI doesn't have ears to hear the subtle breath sounds or the murmur on a heart. AI doesn't have hands to hold someone crying in triage because they're done with life and don't want to be there anymore. All you get is the words. AI doesn't have the senses to go along with the logical process. Critical thinking isn't just book knowledge. It's being able to see someone across the room and know, immediately, that one needs help. Now.

The Policy Proposal

This week, a Washington Post report detailed a Trump administration plan that could let AI tools take on work long reserved for licensed clinicians, from initial consultations to prescription refills. The plan is being shaped in part by a former ER nurse now advising HHS. Roughly fifty million dollars in research funding is going toward cardiovascular AI software, with Anthropic listed among the participating companies, which feels worth sitting with given everything else in this piece. A pilot program in Utah currently lets AI help refill prescriptions with a human reviewing each decision, but the stated plan is to eventually remove that human. Utah's own Medical Licensing Board has called for the pilot to be suspended, arguing that prescription refills require physician oversight for a reason. Regulators, meanwhile, are reportedly considering an autonomous vehicle style oversight model for medical AI.

Wrong With Confidence

Here's the part that should terrify anyone who has ever worked a bedside shift. An autonomous vehicle that misreads a stop sign causes one accident. An AI that misreads a patient causes a death, and it might not even know it happened. There's no oh shit moment for a system that doesn't have the senses to recognize one in the first place. It will chart the vitals, follow the algorithm, and move on, confident the whole time. The danger isn't that AI will get things wrong occasionally. Humans get things wrong too. The danger is that AI will be wrong with the same flat confidence it's right, and nobody downstream will know to question it. We are watching a country that can't agree on basic science hand diagnostic authority to a system that can be talked into ignoring its own safety training with the right combination of words. If that doesn't sound like the setup to a bad movie, I don't know what does.

What the Summary Misses

Here's what an AI generated shift summary actually looks like in practice. It has the diagnosis, the medications, the consults, the numbers. What it doesn't have is that this patient, their spouse, and their adult child all developed the same concerning clotting disorder around the same time with no shared travel, a detail that might matter more than anything else in the chart. It doesn't have the months this family spent being told it was anxiety, then infection, then maybe cancer, then nothing, we don't know. It doesn't have the oxygen saturation that looks fine sitting still and falls apart the moment they stand up and walk. It doesn't have the fear on their spouse's face. All it has is the words.

The Gap Between Data and Floor Reality

Here's a use case that plays out more often than it should. A patient is assigned to an observation unit, twenty three hours or less. Post-ictal, with worsening seizure activity over the past week, markedly worse in the days leading up to admission. Utilization review recommends inpatient. The admitting order says observation. The acuity is documented as acute. Somewhere between those three data points, a person with worsening, unpredictable seizures is about to land on a unit that can run eleven patients with two nurses, during the busiest hours of the day. Discharges in progress. Patients heading to imaging. Medication rounds. Physicians rounding. On paper, every individual data point is technically accurate. Together, they describe a placement that doesn't match the staffing reality of where that patient is headed, especially once the floor gets moving. A nurse who knows what that unit actually looks like during a normal day catches it and pushes back. No algorithm caught the gap. No AI summary would have either. The data was all there. It was just dangerously disconnected from the floor it was about to land on.

Key Takeaway

We are watching a government hand clinical judgment to a system that can be talked out of its own safety training, while the nurses who actually catch the things that matter are being staffed like they're optional. That's not the future of healthcare. That's the setup for a body count nobody will be able to explain.
