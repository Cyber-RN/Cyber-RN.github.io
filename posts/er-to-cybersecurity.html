What 21 Years in the ER Taught Me About Cybersecurity Risk

Triage. Defense in depth. Minimum necessary access. I practiced these concepts for two decades before I knew their security names.

When I tell people I'm transitioning from nursing into cybersecurity, I usually get one of two reactions. Either they think it's an unusual leap, or they nod slowly and say something like "healthcare data is a huge target right now." Both are true. But neither one captures why this transition makes so much sense from where I'm standing.

The ER didn't just prepare me for cybersecurity. It trained me for it. I just didn't have the vocabulary yet.

Triage Is Risk Scoring

Every shift in the ER starts with the same question: who is most likely to die if I don't act right now? Not who arrived first. Not who is loudest. Who has the highest likelihood of serious harm in the shortest window of time.

That's DREAD scoring. In cybersecurity, when we assess a threat, we ask: how much damage could this cause? How many people are affected? How easily can it be exploited? We prioritize based on those answers, not based on which vulnerability showed up first in the scan report.

I've been doing risk-based prioritization my entire career. I just called it triage.

Minimum Necessary Access Is a Clinical Principle

HIPAA has a standard called the Minimum Necessary Rule. When accessing patient information, you're only permitted to see what you need for the specific task at hand. A billing specialist doesn't need a patient's psychiatric history to process an insurance claim. A radiology tech doesn't need medication reconciliation notes to read a chest X-ray.

In cybersecurity this is called the Principle of Least Privilege. Same concept, different domain. You grant users and systems only the access they need. Nothing more. Every extra permission is an attack surface waiting to be exploited.

I spent 21 years enforcing this in clinical practice before I knew it had a security name.

Defense in Depth Is How Hospitals Are Built

A hospital doesn't secure patient safety with a single control. There are policies, training, double-checks, barcode scanning, pharmacist review, nursing verification, and incident reporting systems, all layered on top of each other. No single layer is perfect. The whole point is that when one layer fails, the next one catches it.

In cybersecurity this is Defense in Depth. Firewalls, intrusion detection, endpoint protection, access controls, encryption, monitoring. Layers. If the attacker gets through the firewall, the endpoint protection is still there. If they bypass that, the monitoring catches the anomalous behavior.

Same philosophy. Build layers.

What the Bedside Teaches That the Classroom Doesn't

Security frameworks describe what to do. Clinical experience teaches you what it costs when you don't.

I've been in the room when a system failure delayed critical care. I know what it means when a hospital's EHR goes offline at 2 AM, and I don't mean theoretically. Providers revert to paper. They hunt down allergy histories by phone. They make decisions without complete information.

When I read about ransomware attacks on healthcare systems, I don't see a news story. I see the charge nurse pulling a whiteboard from the supply closet and starting a manual patient tracking system while IT scrambles. I see a nurse, overwhelmed and working from memory, at risk of missing something critical.

That's the real cost of inadequate security. It's why I'm here.

Key Takeaway

The frameworks have names. The principles are not new. If you've spent years making high-stakes decisions under pressure with incomplete information, you already think like a security professional. You just need the vocabulary.
