My Story

I unclick my seatbelt. We're going to jump out quick, grab something, and meet everyone for a triple date. My roommate is driving. Her fiancé is in the back seat. She turns. I see the truck before she does. I throw my arm up in front of her, the way my mom always did to me in the car. Then I go through the window.

I land thirty feet away after being hit by two cars. When I come to, I pat myself down the best I can and start yelling for my roommate. People are already stopping. A woman gets down on her knees in the road and immobilizes my head and neck with her hands. I find out later she's a nurse. A stranger, on her knees, on the pavement, holding my spine still because she knew exactly what could happen if she didn't.

They put me in the ambulance with the man who hit us. He's unconscious. He hit his head on the windshield hard enough to leave a star pattern, the kind I'd later learn as an ED nurse means something is very wrong inside. I'm conscious the whole ride. The medic wants to start an IV. I refuse. I'm not thinking about myself. I keep asking about the other guy.

They take me to Kennestone Hospital, years before it became part of Wellstar. The ED nurses are furious I wouldn't let the medic stick me in the truck. I get an earful. I don't care. I have no idea, lying on that gurney, that I'm looking at my future coworkers. I have no idea I'm about to become one of them.

I walk out the next day with a concussion, a bruised kidney and liver, scratched knuckles, and a small cut above my eyebrow. Nineteen years old and somehow still in one piece.

A year later, I'm back at Kennestone. For something completely different. They say inflammatory bowel disease sometimes has a trigger event, a moment the body never fully recovers from. That accident was mine. It was the catalyst for the rest of my life, just not in the way anyone could have predicted that day on the pavement.

A year of tests followed. Scopes, biopsies, more procedures than I could count, everything that could be shoved into me, was. Eventually I was diagnosed with indeterminate ulcerative colitis. Years later, that would be corrected to Crohn's disease, though by then it almost didn't matter. The label changed. The disease didn't.

Somewhere in the middle of all that testing, I landed back in the hospital. What was supposed to be a weekend of IV steroids and bowel rest turned into eleven days. I was nineteen, alone, and far from my parents, who had moved out of state while I was trying to make it on my own.

There was a nurse named Kim. I'm including this detail because if there's any chance this finds its way to her, or to someone who knows her, I want her to be recognizable. Kim was a young Black woman, working at Kennestone Hospital roughly thirty years ago. She noticed I was alone before anyone else did.

She made a point to keep me occupied. She brought nursing students in and out of my room constantly, full of questions, full of assessments, full of reasons for someone to be there with me. When I needed a central line placed at the bedside for TPN, a procedure done while you're awake, Kim held my hand the entire time.

I wanted to be Kim when I grew up.

When I was finally well enough to go back to the hospital, I brought her a card and a small golden angel pin. She wasn't working that day. I left it for her and never found out if she got it. I never saw her again. She's probably retired by now.

I wish I could find her. I wish I could tell her that a scared, lonely nineteen year old turned into someone who has spent the last twenty one years trying to be exactly what she was to me, for someone else. She was my angel. I just didn't know at the time that she was also showing me who I was going to become.
